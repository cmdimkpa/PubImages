Machine-to-Machine LTE Uplink Simulation of a Novel No-Penalty Proportional-Fair (NPPF) Packet Scheduler

Roderick Ochi and Chidiebere M. Dimkpa



Abstract — This paper introduces and simulates a novel scheduling algorithm that solves the starvation problem of the Proportional-Fair (PF) scheduler by eliminating the penalties it applies to delayed and retransmitted packets. The objective is to improve data transmission rates (throughput) and prevent congestion in a Machine-to-Machine (M2M) Long Term Evolution (LTE) Uplink (UL) scenario. We achieve this by adjusting the PF Channel Quality Indicator (CQI) parameter to favor delayed and retransmitted packets while still prioritizing larger packets. Our experiments were conducted in a web-based M2M LTE Network simulator and the results show that our novel No-Penalty Proportional-Fair (NPPF) scheduler outperforms PF and Round-Robin (RR) schedulers in a number of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) that are critical for M2M data communications over LTE Networks.

Keywords — machine-to-machine; CQI; QoS; scheduling; proportional fair; uplink; starvation; LTE; congestion; HARQ; eNodeB; priority

I. Introduction

With the rise of “Internet of Things” (IoT) and the ubiquity of smart devices with network connectivity, an unprecedented level of Machine-to-Machine (M2M) data traffic is being generated hourly — and this has an impact on the limited existing Long Term Evolution (LTE) networks which also have to handle increasing levels of Human-to-Human (H2H) traffic at the same time. The impact of M2M traffic on LTE networks is assessed in [1].

The authors in [1] describe the characteristics of M2M data traffic — it is bursty, with a large number of communicating devices connected concurrently to the network. The web-based LTE Network Simulator [2] utilized in this research models this behavior of M2M data traffic effectively using live data packets emitted by concurrent logical devices on the Internet. M2M devices typically transmit data of small size compared to H2H traffic, but have a higher potential to congest the network due to the burstiness of the traffic and the large number of devices communicating at the same time. This makes throughput and lower packet delays major focal points of any M2M-optimized packet scheduler in an LTE network.



Figure 1. Flow chart of Proportional Fair (PF) Scheduler [3]

The Proportional Fair (PF) scheduler is widely used in LTE networks. The flowchart above (Figure 1) describes its operation. PF allocates resources based on the user with the highest CQI and in scoring CQI, PF penalizes users with higher than expected packet delays and/or retransmissions (channel quality issues). PF is designed to balance fairness and throughput, but it is known to lead to starvation of “well behaved” users due to its inability to discern users who are backlogged from otherwise normal users experiencing temporary channel quality issues [4]. This is a very significant shortcoming that will lead to lower-than-optimal throughput and network congestion in an M2M LTE Network setting, given the features of M2M communication.

Several research works have suggested improvements to PF that alleviate its starvation problem, however the authors of this paper did not find any that specifically addresses the M2M scenario or displays a high degree of simplicity that will make it more feasible to implement on existing networks already using PF. The authors in [4] describe how a parallel implementation of PF with one “normal” PF instance and another “backlog-unaware” PF instance can solve the starvation problem. However this effectively means using two schedulers — one for removing signaling bias (backlog unaware) and another (normal) for the actual slot allocation. This use of two schedulers can negatively impact overall performance and may be more costly to implement on existing networks. The research in [5] presents a QoS-enhanced proportional fair (QEPF) scheduling algorithm for multi-channel OFDMA networks that has a low complexity (more feasible to implement) and solves the starvation problem, but has not been specifically tested on M2M data traffic.

We propose a highly feasible adjustment to PF that involves reversing the bias against delayed and retransmitted packets by adjusting the CQI parameter. We tested the resulting novel No-Penalty Proportional-Fair (NPPF) scheduler in a simulated M2M LTE Network and we show here in this work that NPPF improves throughput and lowers mean packet delay compared to the traditional PF. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II describes the proposed approach. Section III discusses the experiment setup and results. Finally, Section IV concludes the paper.

It stands to reason that this approach is bound to increase the throughput because previously “shunned” channels and packets will be allocated and transmitted.

To help make this determination and ascertain the degree of performance boost obtained by using the NPPF scheduler in place of PF or RR for a M2M LTE Network, we built a M2M LTE Network Simulation tool [2].

B. Web-based M2M LTE Network Simulator

[2] is an MVC (Model-View-Controller) web application that simulates a M2M LTE Network using a Model, View and Controller. The codebase is published in [6].

The Model (M) contains the primary logic of the Network and is based on the work of the authors in [7]. It was developed using the Python programming language and exposes the LTE Network as a REST (REpresentational State Transfer) API (Application Programming Interface) on the Internet. M has modules that represent the different stages within the lifecycle of a data packet in a M2M LTE Network as shown in Figure 2 below.



Figure 2. Data Packet Lifecycle in LTE Network

The UE Session module registers devices on the eNodeB and creates a channel (also called a session) for the UE which it uses to send IP packets to the eNodeB.



Figure 3. IP Packet Structure

Every IP packet generated in [2] has the above structure. There is a Session ID parameter which serves as a name for the channel. The header section of the IP packet contains information about the packet size, originating UE IP Address, packet creation timestamp, total packet delay and number of retransmissions — while the Payload Bits section contains the actual data transmitted in the packet.

The next module is the MAC packet modulation which essentially breaks up the chunky IP packets into a number of smaller MAC packets that have a maximum size of 1.5Kbits and can be transmitted via a Resource Block (RB) each TTI (see Table 1 for other M2M LTE Network settings). This process is called transcoding and a piece of the original IP packet data is copied to the MAC packet “fragment” based on a transcoding plan. Figure 4 shows the structure of a MAC packet as adopted in [2].



Figure 4. MAC Packet Structure

Notice that there is slightly more information encoded in a MAC packet than in an IP packet; the additional information is necessary to: (i) check the MAC packet for transcoding errors (data copying errors) (ii) orient (sort) and allocate (transmit) the MAC packets.

After the modulation comes the MAC packet profiling. This module essentially decides whether a MAC packet is queued for transmission or retransmitted back to the UE Session module (retransmission). The deciding factor is whether a transcoding error is detected in the MAC packet. Transcoding errors can occur when a channel is noisy or when the MAC layer itself is noisy due to a high level of cell loading [7].





Equation 3. Transcoding Error Condition

Equation 3 above is used to trigger transcoding errors in [2]. In the equation, the aggregate noise level is the product of Ra (channel noise level) and Rb (MAC layer noise level). A transcoding error is likely if the product of these two noise levels is greater than some Bit Error Rate (BER) threshold value (default value for BERthreshold in [2] is 0.2).

The internal method for determining if a transcoding error occured in the generation of a MAC packet is simply to compare the SHA256 hash of the original IP packet data fragment (stored in the MAC packet’s header) with the SHA256 hash of the transcoded bits in the MAC packet. If both identities do not match, the packet is retransmitted — otherwise it is queued for transmission by the scheduler.

A retransmitted packet will preserve its total packet delay and current number of retransmissions when it is sent back into the MAC layer. This ensures that these variables evolve as required with its time in the network and are not reset at any point.

[2] employs a sorting step prior to the actual scheduling (allocation) that is considered part of the function of the scheduler; MAC packets are sorted by channel and the current scheduler “locks” (allocates) an available channel so that other schedulers cannot process it and must find another available channel. This ensures fairness in the distribution of work among the three supported schedulers in [2] (RR, PF and NPPF a.k.a NV).

The View (V) controls the aspects of the simulator that we see when we interact with it. V is a JavaScript library with the following responsibilities: (i) make API calls to the LTE network to collect real-time packet data from existing channels (ii) perform a number of required calculations on the data received, such as the computation of throughput, average packet delay, average scheduler delay and packet loss ratio — among other features, and (iii) update the interactive charts in [2].

The Controller (C) is a collection of network services that essentially drives the network by calling the various network modules at fixed time intervals. C calls the modulation, profiler, sorter and scheduler modules of M every 1ms (TTI - Transmission Time Interval). C also controls concurrency (burstiness) settings for the core M2M aspect of the LTE Network simulation and by default it sends 1 packet every second from 100 logical devices connected to the network.

The resource at [8] can be used to view a color-coded and real-time log of the simulated network activity from any web browser at any time and can be helpful in visualizing or teaching how a live LTE Network operates.

[2] operates as a live network and is always receiving signals from connected users. This allows us to collect and analyse point-in-time measurements of identified Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) — as a method of comparison between the three available schedulers.

To check a real-timevalue of a KPI, we simply click the “Select Scheduler” button to reveal a dropdown from which we select the scheduler of interest from RR, PF and NV (NPPF). Once a selection is made, the View (V) contacts the M2M LTE Network (M) and the real-time data is returned, transformed and rendered on the display.





Figure 5. Screenshot of M2M LTE Simulation Tool

The five KPIs (as seen in Figure 5) are: (i) Throughput (packets), (ii) Throughput (bits), (iii) Average Packet Delay (ms), (iv) Average Scheduler Delay (ms), and (v) Average Packet Loss Ratio (%). A brief explanation of each of these KPIs follows.

Throughput (packets)

Some scheduling algorithms like RR do not use packet prioritization and as such, may push packets through the network without considering the size of the packets. Other scheduling algorithms may be designed to prioritize smaller packets in congested networks and so they will show a higher throughput in number of packets than in the size of the total data transferred, meaning we need to account for number of packets and size of packets separately when analyzing throughput.

Throughput (bits)

Here, we are looking at the total data transferred through the network.

Average Packet Delay (ms)

This is the average time a packet spends in the channel when travelling through the network. It is measured in milliseconds. The higher the average packet delay, the higher the congestion in the network because it implies that packets are not getting out of the network quickly enough. Consider that this is particularly true in M2M LTE Networks because of the burstiness of the packet entry into the network.

Average Scheduler Delay (ms)

This is the speed of the scheduler and it contributes to the mean packet delay in the channel.

Average Packet Loss Ratio (%)

Due to the HARQ limit, packets retransmitted more than 4 times are deemed corrupted and will not be transmitted further (they will be rejected as lost packets). This behavior is simulated in [2] and the ratio of the number of these packets to the number of transmitted packets is called the packet loss ratio. Average packet loss ratio is the packet loss ratio averaged over all existing channels allocated by a given scheduler.

Experimental Technique

Our chosen experimental technique involves using a data collection spreadsheet that enables us to perform experiments and collect data at a rate of 15 trials per scheduler for each of the five KPIs. This means that we are able to collect 75 data points for each scheduler and 225 data points across all three schedulers.

For each KPI, we also obtain the average value across the 15 trials for the scheduler in question and compare that average value with the average value for the NPPF scheduler, obtained in the same way. We use the percentage gain metric to evaluate NPPF’s performance relative to RR and PF — across the five KPI dimensions and report our findings.

B. Simulation Results and Analysis

The raw results of our experiments are available in [9]. The results show that our novel No-Penalty Proportional-Fair (NPPF) scheduler increases throughput by up to 4% when compared to PF (4.2% bit-rate increase and 3.2% packet-rate increase) and by up to 4% also when compared to Round-Robin (3.7% bit-rate increase and 1% packet-rate increase). NPPF is also up to 3% faster than PF while maintaining comparable levels of mean packet delay — meaning that NPPF moves more data faster than PF or RR in M2M data communications.

IV. Conclusion

This paper proposes a no-penalty implementation of the Proportial Fair (PF) scheduling algorithm as a solution to the user starvation problem the conventional PF is known for — especially as it applies to M2M device communications over LTE cellular networks. We apply this no-penalty constraint by reversing the polarity of the cost components in the CQI profit function — to the effect that the resulting novel scheduler prioritizes packet size in order to increase throughput, and will also prioritize (rather than penalize) long-delayed or frequently-retransmitted packets and their channels.

Simulation results uncovered that the novel NPPF scheduler works better than RR or PF under conditions of heavy workload, and that its performance increased with increasing workload. This is ideal for M2M device communications because of the burstiness and concurrency of the communicating devices. We also showed from our experiments that NPPF gives up to 4% more throughput in terms of bit-rate and packet-rate in the M2M LTE network than RR or PF. Finally, we showed that, as a scheduler, NPPF is up to 3% faster than PF.
